Housing Costs and Productivity in England

Here, I present live, interactive visualisations exploring how housing prices influence productivity. My goal is to support data-driven economic policy.

Figure – Median Residential Prices (2000–2023): This chart traces residential property price trends in England, capturing affordability pressures and key shifts around the 2008 financial crisis and post-COVID market surges. It serves as a foundation for interpreting labour mobility and spatial productivity effects.

Figure – Median Prices by Property Type (2000–2023): This chart shows how median property prices in England changed from 2000 to 2023, by property type. Prices increased across all types, with detached homes rising the most, particularly after the COVID-19 period. The growing gap between property types points to shifts in demand, changing household preferences, and rising affordability pressures.

Figure – Regional Productivity Growth (1999–2023): This map shows how regional productivity growth rates vary across the UK, based on GVA per head. The slider below allows comparison across years to see where growth has accelerated or slowed down over time.

Figure – Housing Costs and Productivity: This scatterplot shows the relationship between lagged housing prices and regional productivity (log-transformed). The fitted regression line suggests that higher housing prices are associated with increased productivity.
